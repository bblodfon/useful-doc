Biology

Cell Biology

General

Nucleic Acid

A complex organic substance present in living cells, especially DNA or RNA, whose molecules consist of many nucleotides linked in a long chain.

DNA, Genes, Proteins

Central Dogma

DNA => RNA => protein synthesis (one way)

Exon

After the transcription process, the mRNA is a series of nucleotides that includes regions that code for a protein (exons) and non-coding regions that are called introns. The introns are removed by RNA splicing.

Codon

A three-base sequence of DNA that encodes a single amino acid in the genetic code. There are in total 64 codons. 4 of them are special: one codes for "start" and three for "stop".

Gene Expression

Gene expression is the process that synthesizes a protein out of an RNA strand. It is divided into 2 parts: transcription (happens inside the nucleus) and translation (happens in the cytoplasm of the cell). Watch the video of the whole process as described below:

At transcription, the DNA in the gene is used as a template to make a messenger RNA strand (mRNA) with the help of the enzyme RNA polymerase.

  • Initiation stage: the RNA polymerase enzyme recognizes the promoter region of a gene and binds there (the DNA helix is unwind and opened thus)
  • Elongation stage: the RNA polymerase enzyme slides down the template DNA strand and the complimentary bases pair up (template polymerization)
  • Termination stage: the terminator region of the gene is reached and the DNA strand, the RNA polymerase and the mRNA dissociate from each other

Before the mRNA leaves the nucleus, some modifications are done first to produce the mature mRNA:

  • intros (non-coding regions) are removed (RNA splicing)
  • a 5' cap and a 3' poly-A tail are added (mRNA capping for directionality and protection - start 5', end 3')

At translation, the mRNA nuclueotides (the coding region) will be used to synthesize the protein. The process is as follows:

  • During initiation, the mRNA strand binds to a small ribosomal subunit upstream of the start codon
  • Each aminod acid is brought by a specific transfer RNA molecule (tRNA) which has an anticodon that uniquely identifies a codon and the corresponding amino acid
  • After the initiator tRNA binds to the start codon, the large ribosomal subunit binds to form the translation complex. There are 3 distinct regions in the large ribosomal subunit: E, P, A. The initiator tRNA binds to the P region
  • During elongation, a charged tRNA with an amino acid is brought to the A region and binds to the specified codon through templated polymerization
  • Peptide bond forms between the two amino acids of the P and A regions
  • The complex slides down one codon to the right and the uncharged tRNA of the E region exits
  • A region opens to accept the next charged tRNA
  • This elongation procedure continues until a stop codon is reached
  • Then, a release factor binds to the A site at the stop codon and the polypeptide is released from the tRNA in the P region
  • Lastly the whole complex dissociates

The produced polypeptide (protein) may need to be further modified before its ready to function (PTM).

Promoter

Promoter is a region of DNA that initiates transcription of a particular gene.

Terminator

A transcription terminator is a section of nucleic acid sequence that marks the end of a gene in genomic DNA during transcription. This sequence mediates transcriptional termination by providing signals in the newly synthesized transcript RNA that trigger processes which release the transcript RNA from the transcriptional complex. Release of the transcriptional complex frees RNA polymerase and related transcriptional machinery to begin transcription of new mRNAs.

Isoforms

Alternative splicing, or differential splicing, is a regulated process during gene expression that results in a single gene coding for multiple
proteins. In this process, particular exons of a gene may be included within or excluded from the final, processed messenger RNA (mRNA) produced from that gene. In that case, we have multiple protein variants that come from the same gene and are called isoforms. Isoforms usually perform the same or similar biological functions whereas some of them have specialized/unique functions.

Homolog Gene

A homologous gene (or homolog) is a gene inherited in two species by a common ancestor.

Protein Dimer

Portein dimer is a macromolecular complex formed by two protein monomers (single proteins).

  • A protein homodimer is formed by two identical proteins
  • A protein heterodimer is formed by two different proteins

-omics and -ome

Examples: proteomics, metabolomics, genomics, transcriptomics, proteome, metabolome, genome, transcriptome.

The suffix -omics refers to a field of study. The -ome refers to the totallity of the objects of study in that field. Aim of the study in each case is the collective characterization and quantification of these objects and how they influence the structure, function and dynamics of an organism.

Catalyst

A substance which when added in a biochemical reaction, can largely increase the reaction's rate. It acts repeatedly on the reaction and it is not consumed. The process enabled by a catalyst is called catalysis.

Enzyme

Enzymes are macromolecular biological catalysts - they help accelerate chemical reactions. The molecules upon which enzymes may act are called substrates and the enzyme converts the substrates into different molecules known as products.

Kinases/Phosphorylation

A kinase is an enzyme that catalyzes the transfer of phosphate groups from high-energy, phosphate-donating molecules to specific substrates. This process is known as phosphorylation, where the substrate gains a phosphate group and the high-energy ATP molecule donates a phosphate group.

A phosphatase on the other hand, removes phosphate groups enabling thus the process of de-phosphorylation.

Both phosphorylation and de-phosphorylation can be refered to as form of Post-translational modification (PTM) and are essential to the cell's regulatory network.

Wet-Lab

ChIP

Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) is a experimental technique used to investigate the interaction and map the association between proteins and specific genomic regions such as transcription factors on promoters or other DNA binding sites.

Xenograft

Tissue or organ transplant from a donor of a different species from the recipient. PDX's (Patient derived xenografts) is a term that is used for cancer tissues derived from human patients to mice for research.

MS

MS (Mass Spectrometry) is an analytical lab technique to measure the masses within a sample. It has become an important method for the characterization and sequencing of the proteins.

Cell Singaling

Definition

Part of any communication process that governs basic activities of cells and coordinates all cell actions.
Errors in signaling interactions and cellular information processing are responsible for diseases such as cancer, autoimmunity, and diabetes.

Types

  • Intracrine
  • Autocrine
  • Paracrine (small distance signals)
  • Endocrine (target distant cells - e.g. produce hormones that travel throughout the human body)

Singaling Receptors

Cells receive information from their neighbors through a class of proteins known as receptors. Molecules that activate or inhibit receptors are called receptor ligands - examples are hormones, neurotransmitters, cytokines and growth factors.

Singaling Pathways

Often, the behavior of a chain of several interacting cell proteins is altered following receptor activation. The entire set of cell changes induced by receptor activation is called a signal transduction mechanism or pathway.

Cytokines

A broad and loose category of small proteins used in cell signaling. Cytokines have been shown to be involved in autocrine, paracrine and endocrine signaling as immunomodulating agents.
